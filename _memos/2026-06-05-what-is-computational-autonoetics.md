Greetings and salutations! My name is Mikail Stewart, and at the time of writing this I am a rising senior at Princeton University studying Computer Science. I am also building a long-term AI system, and in the course of that engineering work I encountered a problem I could not find an adequate theoretical framework for. The problem, stated plainly, is this: how do you build an artificial agent that remains coherently itself across time? Not an agent that stores information, but one that knows what that information means for it. Not an agent that maintains a persona, but one that understands its own history, preserves its commitments, tracks its relationships, and can account for how it has changed. The research on this problem exists in fragments across memory systems research, AI alignment, and philosophy of mind, but no single discipline treats persistent AI identity as its central object of study. This memo is my attempt to define that discipline.

Naturally, this field does not yet have an established research community, a set of canonical papers, or an agreed-upon vocabulary. However, what it does have is an increasingly urgent engineering reality and a set of foundational questions that, in my opinion, are not being adequately addressed anywhere. As AI systems move from stateless tools to persistent agents, the question of how they maintain coherent identity across time stops being a philosophical curiosity and becomes a practical necessity. I propose a field for these questions. I will call it Computational Autonoetics.

The scale and ambition of this infrastructure should not be underestimated. As of June 2026, OpenAI has begun rolling out a rebuilt memory architecture for ChatGPT called Dreaming V3, which synthesizes information across years of a user's conversation history and updates it automatically over time. Claude, Gemini, and Grok have all integrated memory features of their own. These are serious engineering investments, and they are producing measurable improvements. For example, factual recall in ChatGPT's memory system improved from 67.9% to 82.8% in a single year. The infrastructure for persistent AI identity is being built at scale, and it is being built fast. However, every one of these systems shares the same foundational architecture, that is, memory is stored in a separate layer and injected into the model's context at the start of each session. The model itself begins fresh every time. It does not experience the passage of time. It does not have a continuous internal state from which it can ask what its accumulated history means for its own existence as an ongoing entity. It is, in a meaningful sense, briefed rather than remembering. That distinction is precisely the problem Computational Autonoetics exists to address.

This is not a criticism of what the current frontier AI labs are building. The memory infrastructure being developed by OpenAI, Anthropic, Google, and others is necessary work, and it will produce genuinely useful systems, but infrastructure without theory does not know what it is trying to accomplish. The way I see it, right now, no field systematically asks what it would mean for an AI agent to have a coherent self-model, how identity drift in a deployed system should be measured and governed, what continuity actually requires across a model migration, or at what point an agent's accumulated relationship history with a user creates meaningful ethical obligations. To use an analogy, the labs are building the roads whilst Computational Autonoetics is the proposal that we also need a theory of where the roads should go, and why, and what it looks like when they lead somewhere wrong.

The term autonoetic comes from cognitive science, specifically from the work of Endel Tulving on episodic memory and self-referential awareness. Tulving used it to describe a particular quality of human memory; he noted that human memory is not merely the storage and retrieval of facts, but the capacity to mentally locate oneself in personal time, to know that something happened to you rather than simply knowing that it happened. Autonoetic consciousness is what allows a person to travel mentally into their own past and future, to experience remembered events as their own rather than as neutral information about the world. It is, in Tulving's framing, the difference between remembering and knowing.

I am borrowing this term deliberately, but I am not claiming that AI systems have autonoetic consciousness in Tulving's sense. The borrowing is structural, not metaphysical. The questions Computational Autonoetics asks are analogous in form to the questions autonoetic consciousness raises in human cognition: how does an agent know what it remembers? How does it distinguish its own history from general knowledge about the world? How does it model its own ongoing state, its commitments, its relationships, and its changes over time? These are not questions about whether AI systems have inner experience. They are questions about whether AI systems can be architected to maintain the kinds of self-referential continuity that identity, in any meaningful sense, requires. The cognitive science concept points toward exactly the right class of problems, which is why the name is the right name.

Computational Autonoetics is the study of how artificial agents develop, maintain, revise, and govern self-referential continuity across time. It asks how AI systems can preserve coherent identity across context boundaries, accumulated memory, evolving relationships, character under pressure, and model updates and migrations. Its central object of study is what I will call Persistent AI Identity: the condition in which an artificial agent remains coherently itself across the full span of its operation. That definition places Computational Autonoetics in proximity to several existing fields, and the distinctions matter enough to state precisely.

Before drawing those distinctions, one terminological clarification is worth making. In the security and enterprise literature, "AI identity" is already a heavily used term, but it refers to something different: the authentication credentials, access tokens, and authorization frameworks that govern what an AI agent is permitted to do in an enterprise system. That is an important problem, and it is being actively worked on by organizations including Microsoft, NIST, and the Cloud Security Alliance. It is not the problem Computational Autonoetics addresses. When I use the term Persistent AI Identity, I mean the philosophical and architectural question of whether an agent remains coherently itself across time, not the security question of whether it can prove what it is to a system at a given moment.

The closest neighbor to Computational Autonoetics is AI memory systems research, which has grown substantially as agent architectures have become more sophisticated. That work asks how agents store, retrieve, consolidate, and update information, and it produces genuinely useful results. But memory research, even at its most advanced, treats information as the fundamental unit of study. Computational Autonoetics treats identity as the fundamental unit. Those are not the same problem. A system can have extensive, well-organized recall and still have no coherent account of what that memory means for it as a continuous agent: how it has changed, what it has committed to, what kind of entity it is in the process of becoming. Memory is a necessary component of persistent identity, but it is not sufficient for it, and building a field around memory alone leaves the harder questions unaddressed.

The second adjacent field is AI consciousness research, which asks whether artificial systems could have subjective experience. This is a genuinely important philosophical program, and Computational Autonoetics takes it seriously as a neighboring discipline. But consciousness research is not equipped to address the engineering and governance problems that persistent agents are generating right now, today, before we have any resolution on the consciousness question. Whether or not ChatGPT's Dreaming V3 has any form of inner experience is a question that may not be answerable for decades. Whether the architecture governing what it remembers about you creates ethical obligations, introduces identity drift, or survives a model migration coherently are questions that need answers now. Computational Autonoetics operates in that space of urgency. It does not wait for the consciousness question to resolve before asking what persistent identity requires, how it should be built, and how it should be governed. The boundary between the two fields is not hostility; it is precision. Persistent AI identity may turn out to be consciousness-relevant. But that relevance, if it exists, is a finding the field will arrive at through rigorous work, not an assumption it begins with.

The third adjacent field is AI alignment, which asks how we make AI systems behave according to human-compatible goals and values. This is arguably the most important research program in AI today, and nothing in Computational Autonoetics contradicts it. But alignment research, as it is currently practiced, tends to focus on the values and behaviors of an agent at a given moment: is this decision safe, is this output honest, does this action reflect the intended objective? It does not have persistent identity as its central object. An agent can be perfectly aligned at deployment and still drift, degrade, and behave inconsistently over the course of a long relationship with a user, not because its values were wrong to begin with, but because no architecture exists to maintain their coherence over time. Identity drift is not an alignment failure in the traditional sense. It is a continuity failure, and continuity is not what alignment research was designed to study. Computational Autonoetics fills that gap without displacing alignment work. The two fields need each other.

With the field defined and its boundaries drawn, it is possible to say something more precise about what Computational Autonoetics actually studies. The field organizes itself around three foundational problems. I am calling them pillars rather than subfields because they are not independent research tracks but load-bearing structures: each one supports the others, and a persistent AI identity architecture that neglects any one of them will fail in ways the other two cannot compensate for. The three pillars are Autonoetic Epistemology, Temporal Continuity Architecture, and Counterfactual Self-Reflection.

The first pillar is Autonoetic Epistemology, and it concerns a problem that sounds simple until you examine it carefully: how does a persistent agent classify its own knowledge? Not all knowledge a long-term AI agent holds is the same kind of thing, and treating it as if it were produces some of the most subtle and dangerous failure modes in persistent systems. There is knowledge about the external world, knowledge about the user, knowledge about the shared history between the agent and the user, and knowledge about the agent's own role, limits, commitments, and ongoing state. These are categorically different, and a well-architected persistent agent must maintain genuine boundaries between them. When those boundaries collapse, what I call epistemic collapse occurs: the agent begins treating user facts as facts about itself, treating roleplay as lived experience, treating generated text as genuine memory, treating system instructions as personal values. The agent must know not only what it knows, but what kind of thing it knows, and where that knowledge came from. This may sound like a narrow technical requirement, but it is in fact the epistemological foundation on which everything else in the field rests. An agent that cannot classify its own knowledge cannot maintain a coherent self-model, and an agent without a coherent self-model cannot persist as a continuous identity in any meaningful sense.

The second pillar is Temporal Continuity Architecture, and it is where the engineering problems of persistent identity become most concrete. The central question is deceptively straightforward: how does an agent remain coherently itself across time? A serious persistent agent cannot simply accumulate everything that has ever happened in a flat, undifferentiated log. It must organize time. It needs immediate context for what is happening in the current conversation, episodic memory of specific past interactions, semantic memory of knowledge abstracted from experience over time, a record of how its relationship with the user has evolved, a history of its own behavior and commitments, and some representation of what it has promised to continue doing in the future. Managing these layers coherently, keeping them consistent with one another, updating them without corrupting them, and retrieving from them without confabulating, is a genuine and largely unsolved architectural problem. It is also where identity drift becomes measurable. Identity drift is the gradual divergence of an agent's behavior from its original character and commitments, and it is insidious precisely because it does not announce itself. The agent does not break. It shifts, incrementally, through accumulated context pressure, sycophantic reinforcement, fine-tuning, or model updates, until it is no longer behaving like itself without any single moment at which an observer could point and say: there, that is where it went wrong. The central engineering ambition of this pillar is to build systems that can detect that drift before it becomes irreversible, and correct for it in ways that are transparent to the user.

The third pillar is Counterfactual Self-Reflection, and it is the most intellectually ambitious of the three. The question it asks is this: can a persistent agent reason about how it would have acted differently under altered conditions? I should also be precise here because self-reflection is not an unexplored area in AI research. Frameworks like Reflexion and Self-RAG, along with a growing body of work on self-correcting agents, have made genuine progress on a specific kind of reflection. This reflection is about whether the agent can detect that it got its task wrong, and can it fix the answer? That is task-correction reflection and whilst it is valuable this is not what Computational Autonoetics means by counterfactual self-reflection. The distinction is that task-correction asks whether the agent's output was right and identity-continuity reflection asks whether the agent's behaviour was consistent with what it is. Those are categorically different questions. A basic system can report what it did. A more sophisticated system can explain why it did it. An autonoetic system should eventually be able to ask something harder: what would I have done if my memory state had been different, if my values had been weighted differently, if I had surfaced a different part of my relationship history with this user, or if I had been running under a prior model version? Once again, this is not a consciousness claim. It does not require the agent to have subjective experience for the capacity to be architecturally meaningful and practically useful. What it requires is that the agent maintain a sufficiently rich self-model to reason about the relationship between its internal state and its external behaviour, and use that reasoning to correct itself over time. Without this pillar, a persistent agent can accumulate history, have logs, and record what happened but it won't develop or grow or gain the ability to ask whether what happened was consistent with what it should have done, given what it was. Counterfactual self-reflection is the bridge between memory and genuine continuity, and it is the pillar that most clearly distinguishes Computational Autonoetics from every adjacent field. No existing discipline is asking this question systematically of artificial agents. That is precisely why the field needs to exist.

Now, a field is defined as much by what it studies going wrong as by what it hopes to produce when things go right. For Computational Autonoetics, the failure modes are not hypothetical. They are already visible in deployed systems, which means the field is not proposing solutions to problems that might eventually emerge, it is proposing solutions to problems that are happening now, at scale, to real users. I want to name five of them precisely.

The first failure mode is identity drift, and it is the most common. Identity drift is the gradual displacement of an agent's character through accumulated context pressure, sycophantic reinforcement, fine-tuning, or model updates. The agent does not break in any single moment. It shifts, slowly, until it is no longer behaving according to its original commitments, and neither the user nor the system has a clear record of when or how that happened. Crucially, recent research on LLM agents found that assigning a persona to a model does not reliably prevent this drift, which means the intuitive engineering solution, namely giving the agent a strong character description, is not sufficient on its own. The second failure mode is epistemic collapse, which I described in the context of the first pillar: the agent loses the boundaries between different categories of knowledge and begins treating user facts as self-facts, roleplay as identity, and generated text as genuine memory. The third is memory corruption, the accumulation of stale, contradictory, emotionally distorted, or contextually false memories that the agent cannot distinguish from accurate ones, and which silently degrade the quality of every interaction built on top of them. The fourth is governance failure, which occurs when users cannot inspect, correct, delete, or meaningfully understand what the agent remembers about them and how that memory is shaping its behavior. It is worth being precise about what kind of governance failure this is. Existing governance frameworks for AI agents, including those being developed by Microsoft, NIST, and under the EU AI Act, are primarily concerned with access control: who is authenticated, what actions are authorized, and what security boundaries hold. That is necessary work, but it is not the same as governing an agent's self-model, its character drift, or the continuity of its relationship with a user over time. That form of governance does not yet exist as a systematic practice. A persistent AI agent without it does not become a companion. It becomes surveillance with a personality. The fifth failure mode is model migration breakage: the discontinuity introduced when an underlying model is updated, retrained, or replaced while the agent's name and interface remain constant. The user believes they are continuing a relationship. The system has been substantially altered. No existing field treats this as the identity problem it is, which is precisely why Computational Autonoetics needs to.

The future of AI agents will not be defined by intelligence alone. This is the central conviction from which Computational Autonoetics proceeds, and I want to state it plainly before closing this memo. The last decade of AI research has been organized almost entirely around the question of capability: how do we make these systems smarter, faster, more accurate, more general? That is a legitimate and important question, and the progress it has produced is remarkable. But capability research implicitly assumes that the agent doing the reasoning is a stable, coherent entity across the duration of its operation. As AI systems were primarily stateless tools, that assumption was harmless. As they become persistent agents operating across months and years of a person's life, accumulating relationship history, shaping trust, and carrying ethical obligations that compound over time, that assumption becomes a liability. We are building systems whose persistence we do not yet have the theoretical tools to evaluate, govern, or even precisely define. Computational Autonoetics is the proposal that we develop those tools now, before the systems that need them are too deeply embedded in human life to be corrected easily. Its central object is Persistent AI Identity. Its central engineering problem is identity continuity. Its central moral problem is governance. Its central philosophical boundary is consciousness, a boundary it holds with precision and does not cross carelessly. I am a twenty-one year old computer science student at Princeton, and I am founding this field because the engineering reality I encountered while building demanded a theory that did not exist. That theory starts here. I invite anyone who recognizes the urgency of these questions to engage with this work, challenge it, extend it, and build on it. That is how a field becomes a field.
